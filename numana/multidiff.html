Multivariate differentiation

The definition

Suppose that you have a function $f:{\mathbb R}^n\rightarrow{\mathbb R}.$ Then we say that it is differentiable at some $\v a\in{\mathbb R}^n$ if locally at $\v a$ the function may be "well approximated" by a linear function passing through $(\v a, f(\v a)).$ Such a function has the form $$ g(\v x) = f(\v a) + D (\v x -\v a), $$ where $D$ is a $1\times n$ matrix (i.e., a row vector). This linear function is what we call the tangent if $n=1$ and the tangent plane if $n=2.$ When this happens, $D$ is called the derivative of $f$ at $\v a.$ It is denoted by $\nabla f$ and called "grad $f$".

If you are curious about what is meant by "well approximated", then here it is: $$ \frac{f(\v x)-g(\v x)}{\|\v x - \v a\|}\rightarrow 0\mbox{ as } \v x\rightarrow\v a. $$ The $\|\cdots\|$ is the length in ${\mathbb R}^n.$

Check that for $n=1$ this is same as our familiar concept of differentiation.

Computing

If $f:{\mathbb R}^n\rightarrow{\mathbb R}$ is differentiable at some $\v a\in{\mathbb R}^n$, then we may compute the derivative easily as follows. $$ \nabla f(\v a)= (f_1(\v a),...,f_n(\v a)), $$ where $f_i$ is the $i$-th partial derivative of $f.$

EXAMPLE:  If $f(x_1,x_2,x_3) = 4x_1x_2 - x_3^2,$ then direct computation shows $$ \nabla f = (4x_2, 4x_1, -2x_3). $$

Differentiaing a quadratic form

We know that derivative of $mx^2$ is $2mx.$ There is an elegant generalisation of this for higher dimension. If $f:{\mathbb R}^n\rightarrow{\mathbb R}$ is of the form $$ f(\v x) = \v x' M \v x $$ for some symmetric matrix $M,$ then $$ \nabla f = 2\v x' M. $$ This is the formula we have used in the soap film example.

Application

Just like the familiar 1-dim derivative, the higher dimensional derivatve also vanishes if $f$ attains a local max/min. We also have an analogous second order derivative check, but we shall not go into that here.

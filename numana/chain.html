Multivariate chain rule

Recall the univariate chain rule: If $f:{\mathbb R}\rightarrow{\mathbb R}$ and $g:{\mathbb R}\rightarrow{\mathbb R}$ are two differentiable functions, then $\frac{d}{dx}f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) g'(x).$

If $f:{\mathbb R}^2\rightarrow{\mathbb R}$ and $g,h:{\mathbb R}\rightarrow{\mathbb R}$, then we have $$ \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x), h(x)) = f_1(g(x), h(x)) g'(x) + f_2(g(x), h(x)) h'(x). $$ We shall not prove it here. But let's see a demonstration.

Suppose that $f(x,y) = (x+y)^2,$ $g(x) = e^x$ and $h(x) = \sin x.$ Then we see that $$ f(g(x), h(x)) = (e^x + \sin x)^2. $$ Differentiating this using the univariate chain rule we get $$ \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x), h(x)) = 2(e^x + \sin x) (e^x + \cos x). $$ To use the multivariate chain rule, note that $f_1(x,y) = f_2(x,y) = 2(x+y),$ and $g'(x) = e^x$ and $h'(x) = \cos x.$ So we have $$ \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x), h(x)) = 2(e^x + \sin x) e^x + 2(e^x + \sin x) \cos x, $$ which is the same as what we got earlier.
